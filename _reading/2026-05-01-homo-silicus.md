The paper that reframed how I think about my own research: treat an LLM as a *computational
subject* you can run economic experiments on, recovering classic behavioral results (endowment
effects, fairness, status-quo bias) from a model never trained to reproduce them. What stays with me
is the methodological move — economics gets a new kind of subject pool — and the open question it
leaves: when is "silicon behavior" a valid stand-in for human behavior, and when is it a confident
artifact of training data? That validity question is exactly where calibration and reliability work
(an AI-safety concern) meets experimental economics.
