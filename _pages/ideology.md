A snapshot of the intellectual scaffolding I'm currently building on — not fixed convictions, but
the lenses I find myself reaching for. I expect this page to change as I read more; that's the point
of writing it down. It's about *ideas*, not biography.

---

### Economics as a theory of incentives, not just markets

The deepest thing my training gave me is a habit: when a system behaves badly, ask what it was
*incentivized* to do, not what it was *intended* to do. Reward misspecification, Goodhart's law, and
specification gaming are the AI-safety versions of problems economists have studied for a century
under names like moral hazard and mechanism design. I increasingly think the alignment problem is,
in large part, an incentive-design problem at a new scale.

### Power and Progress — technology's path is a choice

> *Acemoglu & Johnson.* The trajectory of a technology — who it empowers, whether it augments or
> displaces — is not handed down by the technology itself; it's shaped by the institutions and
> choices around it.

*Why it shapes me:* it's the antidote to technological determinism. Applied to AI, it reframes
"safety" to include the political economy of deployment, not only the behavior of the model. The
future isn't predicted; it's steered.

### Homo Silicus — LLMs as a new kind of subject

> *John Horton and collaborators.* Language models can be treated as computational economic agents,
> opening a new empirical instrument for social science.

*Why it shapes me:* this is the methodological heart of my own work, and it carries a built-in safety
question — when are simulated agents *valid*, and when are we fooling ourselves? Taking that question
seriously is where rigor lives.

### The alignment problem, stated plainly

> *Stuart Russell.* If we build systems that pursue objectives more capably than we do, we had
> better be very sure those objectives are the ones we actually want — and that the system stays
> open to correction.

*Why it shapes me:* it's the cleanest statement of why capability without alignment is dangerous, and
why "just specify the goal" is harder than it sounds. It's the framing I keep returning to.

### Epistemic humility and calibrated forecasting

Borrowing from the forecasting tradition (and from good empirical economics): hold beliefs as
probabilities, track them, and update when reality disagrees. AI is a field with enormous
uncertainty and strong incentives toward both hype and doom. I try to reason in distributions, say
what would change my mind, and treat being wrong out loud as part of the work.

### Scale, neglectedness, and tractability — used carefully

The effective-altruism prioritization lens is part of why I'm drawn to AI safety. I use it as a
*compass, not a calculator*: it's good for noticing where attention is scarce relative to stakes,
and worth holding loosely enough to avoid false precision about deeply uncertain things.
